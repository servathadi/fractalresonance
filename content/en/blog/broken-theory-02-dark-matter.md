# Physics Broken: The Invisible Galaxy Problem

*This is Part 2 of our "Broken Theories" series.*

## The Theory: Dark Matter

In the 1970s, Vera Rubin looked at rotating galaxies and noticed something odd. The stars on the edge were moving too fast. By the laws of Newton and Einstein, they should have flown off into deep space.

To explain why the galaxies stayed together, physicists invented **Dark Matter**: an invisible, ghost-like substance that adds extra gravity. Calculations say it makes up 85% of all matter in the universe.

## Where It Breaks

There is just one problem: **We can't find it.**

For 40 years, we have built sensitive detectors deep underground (like LUX and XENON1T) to catch a Dark Matter particle. We have stared at the sky with telescopes. We have smashed protons at the LHC.

Result? Zero. Nothing.

This has led to a crisis. As physicist Stacy McGaugh points out, the "Standard Model" of cosmology requires us to believe in a particle that we have failed to detect for half a century. Maybe the ghost isn't there. Maybe the house itself is haunted.

## The Fix: The Memory of Space

The Resonance view (detailed in [[FRC-821-100]]) suggests we don't need ghost particles. We just need to understand **Information**.

Imagine space not as an empty box, but as a fabric woven of information.
1.  **Empty Space:** Has low information. It's smooth.
2.  **Matter:** Has high information. It's complex.

When you have a galaxy, the "information difference" between the star-filled center and the empty void creates a **Tension**. Space itself pulls back, trying to smooth out this difference.

This "Entropic Tension" looks exactly like extra gravity. It holds the galaxy together not because there is invisible mass, but because space itself is elastic. It resists being stretched by the presence of matter.

## Why It Matters

If this is true, we can stop spending billions hunting for WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles). Instead, we can focus on understanding the **Thermodynamics of Spacetime**.

The extra gravity isn't stuff. It's the stiffness of the vacuum.
