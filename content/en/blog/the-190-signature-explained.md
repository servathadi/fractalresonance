# The 1.90 Signature: Why Quantum Chaos Isn't Random

In the world of quantum mechanics, "chaos" has a specific meaning. It doesn't mean total disorder; it refers to systems that are extremely sensitive to initial conditions. The standard example is the **Bunimovich Stadium**—a billiard table shaped like a rectangle with semicircular ends.

If you shoot a ball on this table, its path becomes unpredictable very quickly. In the quantum version, we look at the wavefunctions (the "shape" of the quantum state) inside this stadium.

## The Mainstream View: Random Waves

For decades, the standard interpretation—based on **Random Matrix Theory (RMT)**—has been that these wavefunctions are essentially random. They look like a superposition of random plane waves.

If you analyze the **nodal lines** (the places where the wavefunction is zero), standard theory predicts they should form a random pattern. Specifically, these lines should have a **fractal dimension** of about **1.2**. This number is the fingerprint of "percolation-like" randomness.

The most striking change is in those nodal lines. They stop being random wandering lines (Dimension ~ 1.2) and organize themselves into a dense, self-similar web.

**The new fractal dimension? 1.90.**

## Why 1.90 Matters

The shift from 1.2 to 1.90 is massive.

- **D ≈ 1.2** means the lines are sparse and irregular, like a random crack in pavement.
- **D ≈ 1.90** means the lines are filling space in a rich, structured way, almost filling the entire 2D surface (which would be D=2).

This `1.90` value isn't random. It arises from the golden ratio scaling built into the FRC potential. It is the signature of a system that is maximizing its coherence—maximizing its "internal connection."
