# **Chapter 1 — Against Closure**


The Overture left us with a strange claim, and I want to spend this chapter earning it slowly, because everything else rests here.

The claim is that the closed system — the sealed box, the thing cut off from the world — does not exist. Not that it is rare, or hard to build, or only approximate. That it is absent. That in the whole history of looking, no one has ever found one.

This sounds, at first, like the kind of thing that cannot be true. Surely some things are closed. The sealed jar. The thermos. The locked room. The universe itself, perhaps, taken all together. We have a deep intuition that the world comes in pieces, and that a piece can be walled off, set apart, sealed up. Let us take that intuition seriously, and look at it, and see whether it survives the looking.


Pick the best closed system you can think of, and let us see what happens to it.

Take a thermos of hot coffee, a good one, the kind that keeps your drink warm for hours. For a while it seems to do exactly what a closed system should: the heat stays in, the cold stays out, the inside is sealed off from the room. If anything in ordinary life is closed, this is.

But put your hand on it after an hour, and it is cooler than it was. The heat has been leaving the whole time — slowly, through the walls, no matter how good they are. There is no material that stops heat completely. There is only material that slows it. Give it a day and the coffee is room temperature; the inside has come into balance with the outside, which means the wall was never truly a wall. It was a delay. Every insulated boundary in the world is like this — not a barrier but a slowing, not a seal but a lag. The thermos does not close the system. It only makes the leak take longer.

Now go the other way and seal harder. Pump out the air, make a vacuum flask, wrap it in mirror to throw back the radiant heat. You can slow the leak enormously. But you cannot stop it, because heat finds every road out — through the thin glass neck, through the radiation no mirror fully returns, through the simple contact of the thing with the table it sits on. And even if you could somehow stop the heat, you could not stop gravity, which I will come back to, because gravity turns out to be the thing that finishes this argument. Seal as hard as you like. The leak gets slower. It never reaches zero.


Here is the move that makes it certain, and it costs nothing but a willingness to let the clock run.

Everything I just said depends on time. The thermos looks closed for an hour and is plainly open after a day. So the word “closed” was always hiding a clock inside it. When we call something closed, we mean closed for now — closed on the timescale we happen to care about. Stretch the timescale and the closure dissolves, every single time.

So let us stop caring about an hour, or a day, or a life, and let the clock run all the way out.

Take the most sealed thing you can imagine and give it the full age of the universe. The jar cracks. The seal rots. The glass itself, given enough time, flows and fails. And long before the end of things, the sun that warms this whole story will swell into a red giant and swallow the inner planets and scatter every sealed jar that ever sat on every shelf into a thin hot gas spread across the dark. There is no container that survives this. Not one. Every seal is a seal that has not yet broken. Every closed thing is an open thing filmed too briefly to catch it opening.

And this gives us a clean way to see the truth, maybe the cleanest there is. Take time out of the picture. Do not watch a short slice and ask whether the box holds. Instead, lay the whole of existence out at once — all of space, across all of time — and look for the boxes. There are none. A box is a thing with a sealed boundary, and across the full reach of time there are no sealed boundaries, only walls in the slow act of failing. Remove the short clock we live by, look at space across the whole of duration, and what you see is not a crowd of separate sealed things. You see one connected flow, leaning into itself everywhere, with no true edge anywhere inside it.


I have been talking about closure failing. There is something underneath that, deeper than the failing of any particular seal, and it is the real floor of this book.

It is not only that boxes leak. It is that the destination the box was built to reach does not exist.

Think about what the sealed box was for, in our imagination. We seal a thing off so that, left alone, it will settle. Come to balance. Come to rest. The hot and the cold even out, the motion dies down, and the system arrives, finally, at stillness — quiet, finished, at peace. That picture of a final rest sits underneath an enormous amount of our thinking. It is the heat death of the universe; it is equilibrium; it is the idea that things left alone wind down and stop.

And stillness, it turns out, is forbidden. Not hard to reach. Forbidden.

Let me come at this gently, because it runs against something that feels like common sense. We tend to think rest is the natural state and motion is the thing that needs a cause. A ball lies still until something kicks it. So absolute rest feels like the easy default, the thing everything would do if just left alone.

But the deepest physics we have quietly turns this around. There is a law — it goes by the name of the uncertainty principle — that says a thing cannot have, at the same time, a perfectly fixed place and a perfectly fixed motion. The two cannot both be exact at once. Now ask what perfect rest would actually require. It would require a finite, localized thing — a thing that is somewhere, with a definite place — to also be moving exactly zero: a fixed place and a fixed motion, both perfectly sharp, at the same time. That is precisely the pair the law forbids. So a finite, localized thing cannot be both perfectly placed and perfectly motionless. And so the conclusion falls out, quiet and strange and unavoidable: such a thing cannot be perfectly at rest. Motion cannot be switched all the way off.

You can chill a thing toward absolute zero, the coldest temperature there is, and it does not go still. It keeps a last irreducible tremble that cannot be removed — because removing it would mean reaching the perfect stillness the law does not allow. Physicists have a flat name for this leftover motion: zero-point energy. The name is dull. What it means is not. It means there is no rest anywhere in the world. The stillness we imagined at the bottom of everything, the quiet floor under all the motion — it is not there. Go all the way down and you do not find rest. You find a tremble that never stops.

So turn the common-sense picture over. There is no still floor that motion departs from. The tremble is the floor. A thing is not a stillness that sometimes moves. A thing is a motion that sometimes holds still enough, on our timescale, to look like a thing.


Now put the two findings side by side, because they are really one finding seen twice.

No system is ever closed — every wall leaks, and given time every seal fails. And no system ever comes to rest — the deepest law forbids perfect stillness. These are not two separate facts that happen to agree. They are one fact. The sealed box was supposed to hold a thing apart so it could settle into rest. But there is no holding-apart to be had, and there is no rest to settle into. The box fails to seal, and even if it could seal, it would have nowhere to arrive. Its whole reason for being — undisturbed approach to stillness — stands on two impossibilities at once.

Take both away and what is left is simple, and it is the thing you felt in the Overture, in your own breath. The unsealed. The flow that never closes and never stops. Not as one picture we might choose among others. As the only picture the world actually allows.


I need to be careful here, more careful than anywhere else so far, because the strength of everything ahead depends on saying exactly what I am claiming and exactly what I am not.

Everything I have said is about the world of things — the world of matter and motion, the world we live in and measure, the world physics calculates. There, among moving things, closure is impossible and stillness is forbidden. That is the claim, and the whole book will lean on it.

It is not a claim about whatever may lie beneath the world of things — some ground or source that the moving world is drawn out of. People have pointed at such a ground for as long as there have been people, under many names, and the wisest of them have mostly had the honesty to say that almost nothing can be said about it. There may be a stillness there — but if there is, it is not the stillness of a stopped clock, not a thing that was moving and then halted. It would be something else entirely: not a rest the world falls into, but a ground the world rises from, before motion is even a question. About that, here at the start, I will say almost nothing, because almost nothing is what I can honestly say. I am not going to name it yet, or build on it. We will circle back to it, carefully, much later, once we have earned the right to speak of what we cannot measure. For now I only want to draw the line clearly: inside the world of things, there is no closure and no rest. What lies beneath the world of things is not my foundation. The flow is my foundation, and the flow is never closed.

It is the difference between a claim I can defend and a claim I cannot, and the whole book depends on keeping the two clear. Thinkers who loved the flow have ruined the insight before by overreaching — by sliding from “everything in the world moves” to “there is nothing anywhere but motion, all the way down and all the way back,” which is a far bigger claim than anyone has the standing to make. I am making the smaller claim, and I am making it without a flinch: in the world we live in and measure, nothing is sealed, and nothing is at rest.


If we walk on from here, a few honest worries rise up to meet us. I would rather walk straight through them than around them, because each one, looked at squarely, makes the ground firmer instead of softer.

The first worry is that this is all just words. Fine, the skeptic says — no system is perfectly closed. But things can be closed enough. The thermos is closed enough to keep your coffee warm through lunch. Insisting that the seal is never perfect is just fussing over a technicality that never mattered.

And for the thermos at lunch, the skeptic is completely right, and I want to grant it plainly. For many purposes, treating a thing as closed is a fine approximation, and no one should pretend otherwise. But notice what the word “enough” is quietly doing. Closed enough for this purpose, over this stretch of time. The approximation is honest exactly as long as it remembers it is an approximation — as long as it keeps the clock in view and admits the leak is real and only slow. The trouble starts when we forget. When we take the box we drew for convenience and decide the world is really made of boxes, with the leaks as nuisances to subtract away. Then we have mistaken a tool for the truth. And when we carry that mistake into the deepest questions — the direction of time, the fate of the universe, what happens when we measure a quantum — the leak is not a technicality anymore. It is the whole story.

The second worry runs deeper, and it deserves real respect. If nothing can be closed, the skeptic asks, then how is science even possible? Science works by sealing things off — by isolating a variable, controlling the conditions, holding everything else still so one effect can be seen. Have you not just kicked the legs out from under the very method that would test anything you say?

I think, looked at closely, the opposite is true. When a careful experimenter isolates her system, she is not claiming to have cut it off from the universe. She is claiming something smaller and far more honest: that the influences she has not controlled are too small and too steady to matter for the thing she is watching. That is a statement about sizes, not about seals, and it sits perfectly well beside everything I have said. In fact it needs it. Because she can never assume the box is truly closed, she has to keep asking what she failed to control, what leaked in, what she missed — and the history of science is largely the history of discovering that something everyone had waved away as negligible was not negligible at all. A science that believed in real closure would stop asking. It would stop looking for the hidden influence, because it would think the box was sealed. It is because nothing is ever truly closed that the good experimenter never stops checking the walls. The axiom does not break the method. It names the carefulness the method already lives by.

The third worry is the sharpest, and I have saved it because answering it is the firmest ground in the chapter. The skeptic says: you have made your claim unfalsifiable. You say no one has ever observed a closed system — but to observe anything is to interact with it, and to interact is to be coupled to it. So of course you never see a closed system; you have defined closure as the one thing that could never be detected. A truly closed system would be invisible by your own logic. You have made your claim true by definition and therefore empty.

This is the one to slow down for, because if it holds, this whole chapter is just a clever tautology. It does not hold — and the reason has two parts. The first is the deeper one: perfect isolation is not a condition the world is ever found in. It is a modeling ideal — a thing we draw to make the arithmetic clean — not a state any region of nature has been observed to occupy. The closed system is a tool of calculation that we mistook for a kind of object. The second part is the most vivid illustration of the first, the one that is hardest to wave away, and it is gravity.

Almost every way that things touch can, in principle, be blocked. You can shield against electricity with a metal cage. You can insulate against heat. You can block light with a wall. If those were the only ways things reached each other, then maybe the skeptic would be right — maybe closure would be merely undetectable, a thing we rule out by definition rather than by looking. But there is one way of reaching that cannot be blocked by anything, and that is gravity. There is no gravity shield. There is no material you can stand behind, no cage you can build, no arrangement of matter that severs the coupling of mass to mass. It passes through everything. Stand in the most sealed room ever built and you are still coupled to the Earth; leave the Earth and you are coupled to the Sun; leave the Sun and the galaxy holds you; leave the galaxy and you are still drawn, faintly, toward the great far-off gatherings of mass beyond it. You can stand at a point where the pulls happen to cancel and feel no net tug at all — but the coupling has not gone anywhere; you are still tied to every mass whose pulls are balancing out around you. No shield deletes the coupling to the rest. There is no place, and no way, to be gravitationally alone.

So closure is not undetectable after all — and this is why it is an illustration of the deeper point rather than a separate proof. A truly closed system would have to be cut off from everything, gravity included; and gravity, at least, has no off switch and no wall that stops it, so the isolation the closed-system model imagines is not a state we can even approach all the way, let alone reach. The skeptic said we could never detect a closed system. But we do not need to detect one to settle the question. We only need to notice that everything, everywhere, is still being pulled — and being pulled is being coupled, and being coupled is being open. The closed box is not a thing that hides from our instruments. It is a thing that was never in nature to begin with, only on our chalkboards.

And the point turns sharper still when we ask what is doing the coupling. We have learned, in the last century, that most of what shapes gravity is nothing we can see. The matter that shines, the matter our instruments can touch through light — the ordinary stuff that you and the stars and this book are made of — is a small share of the gravitationally relevant reality out there. The great majority is dark: mass we detect only by its gravity, that gives off no light at all, and beyond even that, something stranger that drives the whole universe apart, and that we understand less. Set that beside the skeptic's challenge and watch what happens to the burden of proof. When someone declares a system closed, they mean closed against the influences their instruments can register — the small, shining fraction. But the system stays gravitationally coupled to the vast dark remainder their instruments cannot register, and that coupling is real, and ever-present, and impossible to shield, whether or not anyone can measure it. Their "closed" system is closed only against the few percent of the world that happens to glow. The dark majority of gravitationally relevant reality goes on shaping it, unseen, unblocked, unbroken.

So it was never my job to prove that things are open. It was always their job to prove that even one thing is closed — that perfect isolation, the clean ideal of the model, is somewhere instantiated in the world. That proof has never been given. The burden sits with whoever would claim a real closed system, and the whole pulling, mostly-dark universe stands in the way of their giving it.


So we arrive, having walked through the worries rather than around them, at a place we can stand.

There are no closed systems in the world we live in. There is no rest — not as something rare, but as something the deepest law forbids. What there is, instead, is what you felt at the very start, in the simple fact that you are reading this and breathing as you read: a world whose parts stand open everywhere, leaning into one another everywhere, taking in and giving out, while the whole never finishes and never stops. The sealed box and the final stillness were tools we drew to make the arithmetic easier, and somewhere along the way we forgot they were tools and let them become our picture of what is real.

This book sets out to draw a truer picture. To take the unsealed flow as the starting point — not the exception, not the complication, but the plain fact — and to ask, patiently, what follows from it. And what follows is large, larger than so simple a beginning would seem to promise. If nothing is ever finished, we will have to learn to think of being itself as a kind of flowing, and of things as the shapes the flow holds for a while. If nothing is ever cut off, we will have to look again at whether anything is ever truly random, or truly alone. If a mind is a motion and not a thing, then thinking is something the flow does, and we will have to ask where else, and at what scale, it might be doing it. And if everything that lasts is a flow that cannot stop, then the turning shapes such things fall into — the spiral, the vortex, the whirl that comes back and yet moves on — will turn out to be not decoration but necessity.

Each of those is a chapter. Each grows from the box that was never there. We begin where it is right to begin: with what it could possibly mean to be, in a world where nothing is ever sealed and nothing is ever still.
