# **Chapter 4 — Determinism Without Closure**


Chapter 3 left us with a question that has been waiting since the start. If nothing is ever sealed — if every thing is a flow open to every other, leaking and pulling and exchanging without stop — then is anything ever truly cut loose? Is anything ever random?

This is not a small question. It sits under everything. Whether the world is, at bottom, lawful or chancy decides what kind of place we are living in. And the answer most people carry is that the world is chancy at the bottom — that physics found, in the last century, real randomness woven into the floor of things. Quantum events, we are told, are not just unpredictable to us. They are undetermined in themselves. The universe rolls dice.

I want to look at that claim slowly, because I think the axiom of this book puts a crack in it. Not a victory over it. A crack — and an honest account of how wide the crack is, and what it would take to close it either way.


First, let us be clear about what randomness would have to mean.

There is a weak kind of randomness, and nobody argues about it. A coin flip is “random” in the weak sense — not because the coin escapes the laws of motion, but because we cannot track the thumb, the air, the spin, the table, finely enough to call it. The coin is fully determined. The flip obeys mechanics exactly. It is only random to us, because our knowledge runs out before the causes do. This is randomness as ignorance, and it threatens nothing. The causes are all there; we just cannot see them.

The strong kind is different, and it is the one that matters. Strong randomness says: the event has no full cause. Not a cause we cannot see — no cause at all. The quantum event, on this reading, is not determined by anything, hidden or visible. It simply happens, uncaused, out of nothing that fixed it. The dice are not merely unread. They are genuinely loaded by nobody.

It is this strong randomness the axiom has trouble with. And the trouble is simple to state.


A truly uncaused event would have to be cut off from everything that could have caused it.

Think about what “no cause” requires. If the event were connected to the rest of the world — coupled, piped, in exchange with the flow around it — then that flow would be acting on it, shaping it, feeding into what it becomes. To be genuinely uncaused, the event would have to be sealed away from all of that. Severed. A little island in time, with nothing flowing in to determine it. Otherwise the flowing-in is the cause, and the event is not uncaused after all.

But we spent Chapter 1 on exactly this. There are no islands. Nothing is severed. Nothing is sealed off from the rest of the flow — not a particle, not an event, not anything, because the one coupling that cannot be shut off, gravity, reaches everywhere, and the world is one connected current with no true cuts in it. A genuinely uncaused event would be a closed system in time — a happening with no inflow. And closed systems do not exist.

So the axiom presses against strong randomness. If nothing can be severed from the flow, then nothing can be genuinely uncaused — provided that to be uncaused is to be severed.

There is a weak joint in that argument, and it is exactly where to press. Is it really true that an uncaused event must be a severed one? A determined critic can resist this. He can say: an event might be embedded in the unsealed flow — coupled to everything, pulled by everything, never severed at all — and yet still not be fully fixed by what came before it. The flow pours into it, shapes the odds, sets the stage — and then the event does something not wholly settled by that inflow, something with a genuine looseness in it, without ever being a closed island. On that picture, openness and indeterminacy live together: the event is open, and still not determined. If that is coherent — and I cannot prove it is not — then my move from "nothing is severed" to "nothing is uncaused" does not strictly follow. The axiom forbids the closed event; it does not, by itself, forbid the open-but-loose one.

I take that seriously. So I claim something smaller than proof: that the open-but-loose event is a strange hybrid, harder to make sense of than it first looks — for if every thread of the flow pours into the event and shapes it, it is not clear what the leftover looseness could be made of, or where it could come from, that is not itself another thread of the flow. The cleaner reading, the one with fewer moving parts, is that the looseness is only apparent — fine-grained causation we cannot follow, not genuine slack in the world. So the axiom does not force determinism; it favors it, by making randomness pay a cost — the cost of explaining a looseness that has no flow to come from. A lean, then, not a proof. The event that looks random is an event whose causes run out into the whole connected flow — a flow so vast and so finely woven that we cannot trace the threads, but a flow that is there, piping into the event, fixing what it becomes. The randomness, on this reading, is the strong-looking face of the weak kind. It is ignorance again — not the small ignorance of the coin, but the total ignorance of a cause that spreads out into the entire universe and cannot be followed. Determined, but undetectable. Caused by everything, and so traceable by no one.

And I want to be careful that this “determined” is not heard as the old clockwork — the world as a film already shot, every frame waiting in a sealed canister to be played. That picture is itself a closure, a box drawn around the future, and the axiom forbids it as surely as it forbids the uncaused spark. The determination I mean is stranger and lighter than that. The next event is fixed by the whole connected flow, yes — but it is not written anywhere in advance, not sitting finished in some register the present is merely reading off. It has no existence until the flow arrives at it; the only way to find what it is, is for the current to run forward and resolve it. Fixed, and yet unwritten — determined the way the next figure of an unending calculation is determined, certain to come out one way, but not stored ahead of the working, reachable only by doing the work. So the world is not a sealed future being unrolled. It is a determination with no closed box anywhere in it — caused by everything, written nowhere, and made only at the moving edge where the flow comes to its point.


Here the argument runs out, and I will not push past it.

I have not proven the world is determined. I have shown that the axiom leans that way — that “no closed systems” sits much more comfortably with hidden determination than with genuine, uncaused chance. But leaning is not proving, and here is exactly where the argument runs out.

The person who believes in strong randomness can say: you cannot show me the hidden causes. You are telling a story in which every quantum event is secretly piped by the whole universe — but you cannot trace one thread of that piping, not one, and a cause you can never in principle detect is not so different from no cause at all. Your determinism is as invisible as my randomness. Neither of us can show the other the goods.

And he is right that we are stuck. At the precision we can measure today, the two stories make the same predictions. The quantum event looks exactly as random to my “secretly determined” reading as to his “genuinely uncaused” one. Nothing we can currently measure tells them apart. It is a tie.

It is a tie, and that is the honest position: the axiom makes determinism the more natural reading, and strong randomness the more awkward one — because randomness needs a severing the axiom forbids — but the matter is not settled, and at present precision it cannot be settled, and anyone who tells you it is settled, in either direction, is reaching past the evidence.

And yet the tie is not perfectly even, and there is one thread of evidence worth weighing, lightly, before we move on — because it tilts the table, even if it does not flip it. It comes from the people who need real randomness and cannot make it. A computer, however vast, cannot generate a truly random number. It can only compute — follow rules — and rules produce sequences that look random but are not, that a sharp enough analysis unwinds back to their seed. This is not a small engineering nuisance; it is a known limit. So when randomness is genuinely needed — for the keys that guard secrets, where a predictable "random" number is a broken lock — it cannot be calculated. It has to be harvested. The machines reach outside their own computation and sample the physical world: thermal noise in a resistor, the timing of radioactive decay, the jitter of electrons. They go to the thermodynamic floor and scoop up what looks, to them, like chaos.

Now weigh what that means — and weigh it carefully, because the easy version of this argument is wrong and worth not making. The easy version says: computers cannot make randomness, therefore the world is determined. That does not follow, and a critic would be right to swat it; the limit of a computer is a fact about computation, not a proof about the world. So let me make the smaller, truer claim, the one that actually belongs to this book. A computer is a closed formal system — a thing running rules inside its own boundary — and a closed formal system cannot manufacture openness from within itself. It cannot reach, by pure calculation, anything its rules did not already contain. So when real chance is needed, the machine has to do the one thing its closure forbids: open a window to the physical world and let something in. It samples the thermal floor, the decay, the jitter — it reaches outside. And here is the lesson, which holds no matter which way the deeper question falls: whether what comes through that window is genuine indeterminacy or causation too dense to trace, the chance did not come from the closed machine. It entered through the opening. The sealed system produces no novelty; novelty enters only where the seal is broken. That is not a proof that the world is determined — it is the axiom of this book showing up in the one place you would least expect it, inside the logic of computation: closure manufactures nothing new, and everything that enters, enters through the open. I hold the determinism itself as a lean, not a proof, exactly as before. But the deeper pattern is firm, and it is ours: chance is not made inside the closed box. Like everything else in this book, it comes in only through the opening.

So the tie holds, but it is not weightless. The axiom leans toward determinism; the physics of where randomness actually comes from leans the same way, a little; and the matter still waits, as it must, for the finer instrument.


But a tie is not the end of the story, and the reason is what makes this a real claim and not just a preference.

The tie breaks the moment someone can measure finely enough.

The two readings agree only at the resolution we have now. They do not agree forever. If the world is secretly determined — if the apparent randomness is fine-grained causation we cannot yet follow — then a sharp enough instrument would start to see the determination. The “random” event would show structure, correlation, a thread of cause where we used to see only dice. And if the world is genuinely, strongly random, then no instrument, however sharp, would ever find such structure, because there would be none to find. The dice would stay dice all the way down, forever.

That is a real difference. It is just a difference we cannot yet reach. The instrument that would settle it does not exist — not because it is forbidden, but because we have not built it. And someday, perhaps, we will. A finer probe, a better experiment, a machine that reads the quantum floor at a resolution we cannot touch today. On that day the tie breaks, and one of us is shown to be wrong, and it might be me.

I am not saying "the world is determined and you cannot check." I am saying "the world is probably determined, because the axiom leans against the looseness that randomness needs — and here is the experiment that would prove me wrong: build the finer instrument, and if the floor stays random all the way down, no matter how deep you look, then my determinism is beaten." But I should be exact about how much that costs me, because there are two ways to lose here and they are not the same size. If the floor is merely *undetermined* — loose, not fixed by the inflow, yet still coupled to everything — then I lose the determinism and keep the axiom: every part can stand open to every other, leaning into the rest, and still not be pinned by that leaning. The box is not real in that case; the event is still coupled, still unsealed, just not fixed. The box is real only the other way: if the random event is shown to be *severed* — cut loose from the flow entirely, uncaused with no inflow at all. That is the closure the axiom forbids, and finding it would beat not just the determinism but the whole book. So the wager has two stakes, and I name both: bottomless randomness costs me determinism for certain, and costs me the axiom only if that randomness is severance and not mere looseness. Either way the claim wagers something real. It names the measurement that would kill it — and says exactly which part dies.


I should say where this argument has company, because I am not the first to make it.

A serious physicist, David Bohm, spent much of his life building exactly this kind of picture — a reading of quantum mechanics in which the apparent randomness is incompleteness, in which hidden variables, woven into an undivided whole, determine what looks like chance. His view is a minority one and contested, and I do not lean on his authority. I name him because a first-rate physicist looked at the same dice and concluded, on grounds of his own, that the world beneath them is whole and connected and very likely determined. The idea that quantum randomness might be the surface of a deeper, undivided order is not a thing I invented to save my axiom. It was already there, in the physics, argued by people who knew the mathematics far better than I do.

And there is a piece of measured physics here that cuts the other way from how it is usually told. There is a theorem — Bell's — and a long line of experiments behind it, careful enough to win a Nobel Prize, that are often said to have killed the dream of hidden determination beneath the quantum dice. They did no such thing. What they ruled out is *local* determination — the idea that the hidden causes are tidy little signals passing from place to place, no faster than light, each event fixed only by what is near it. That picture is dead; the experiments killed it. But read what they put in its place. They showed that distant events are connected in a way no local signal can explain — that two particles, once coupled, stay bound across any distance, their outcomes laced together as if the space between them were not the barrier we took it for. The world they revealed is *not* locally separable. The severing we imagined between far-apart things is not there.

Now set that beside the axiom of this book. I never claimed the determination was local. I claimed the opposite from the first page: nothing is severed, everything is coupled through the one connected flow, the cuts we draw between things are conveniences and not true walls. So the experiments that embarrassed the local hidden-variable picture do not embarrass this one. I want to be precise about what they do and do not give me, because it is easy to claim too much here. They do not prove the determinism — that stays a lean, exactly the lean of this chapter, and no experiment yet performed settles it. What they establish is narrower and is the thing I actually need: that the world is not separable, that distant things stay bound in a way no local-signal story can explain, that the severance we imagined between far-apart things is not there. That is the first premise of this book — nothing severed — and it is now measured, carefully enough to have won a Nobel Prize. So I lean on the experiments for the premise, not for the conclusion: they give measured support to one major case where separability fails — the entangled pair, whose outcomes stay laced together across distance — and they leave the determinism where I left it, as the more natural reading of a world that cannot be cleanly cut. I should be exact about the reach of this. Bell's experiments do not measure the book's whole axiom; they do not show that *every* pair of things everywhere is non-separable. They show it decisively in one important place — the quantum correlations of once-coupled particles — and that single, well-measured case is enough to break the assumption that separateness is the default, the safe background picture into which couplings are added. The book's axiom asserts more than any experiment has shown; but here is one hard case, carefully measured and Nobel-honored, where the severance we imagined is demonstrably not there. What the physicists found expensive to accept, because they began from a world of separate local things and had to give the separateness up, this book began without ever holding. The non-locality that is a steep price to a separable worldview is simply the axiom, measured. Bohm's reading survives those experiments for exactly this reason: his hidden order is whole and non-local, woven through the undivided, not a crowd of local signals. The price the physics demands — give up local separability — is the one thing this book never owed, because it never bought the separateness in the first place.

The formal version of all this — the precise statement, the place where the prediction lives and could be checked — is in the back of the book, with the rest of the testable machinery. It is a distinct bet, and a losable one: if it loses, this chapter's wager loses with it. The two are nested, not walled — the predictions are this argument reaching down to where an instrument can touch it. Here, in the philosophy, the claim is only this much: the axiom leans hard toward determination, because randomness needs a cut the world does not allow.


So where does that leave chance?

Right where the axiom puts it — real the way the horizon is real. The horizon is a true feature of how the world looks from where you stand. Walk toward it and it moves; it is not a wall out there at the edge of things. It is the place your seeing runs out. Randomness, I think, is like that. It is a true feature of how the world looks from inside our limited reach — the place our tracing of causes runs out. Real, in that sense. Reliable. You can build on it, calculate with it, trust it to behave. But not a wall at the edge of the world. Not a true cut in the flow. The place our knowledge ends, mistaken, understandably, for the place the causes end.

And I hold even that with an open hand, because of the tie. Maybe the horizon is a wall after all. Maybe the dice are really uncaused, and the world has genuine cuts in it that my axiom cannot allow, and someday the finer instrument will find them and I will have to give up either the randomness claim or the axiom that forbids it. I do not think so. The whole weight of “no closed systems” leans the other way. But I have told you exactly what would beat me: a claim that leans hard on a solid axiom, names its own kill, and waits for the instrument that does not yet exist.

One question is left, and it is the one a reader feels in the chest before the head. If the flow determines everything — if every event is piped by the whole connected current, fixed though no one can trace it — then what becomes of choosing? Am I a puppet of the flow, my sense of deciding only a story I tell after the current has already moved my hand?

I do not think so, and the reason is in the shape of the determination, not against it. The flow does not push you around from outside, the way a hand works a puppet. You are not outside it to be pushed. You are one of the places it resolves — a knot where the whole current gathers and becomes one definite thing rather than another. The deciding is not something done to you by the flow. The deciding is the flow, narrowing to a point, at you.

Watch where it happens. A car drifts into your lane, and in the half-second you have, either you see it and pull the wheel, or your attention is elsewhere — on the phone, on a worry, on nothing — and the moment closes over you. The difference between the dodge and the crash is not muscle. It is where your attention was resting when the fork arrived. Attention sits at the edge this book keeps circling — the live edge between holding a shape and losing it — and where it falls is what tips the event one way rather than the other. That fall of attention is what choosing is. Not a faculty bolted onto the machine. The live settling of a fork the whole flow has loaded but not yet landed.

Here is the place to be careful, because it is easy to hear more in it than is there. The fork is loaded — heavily. Everything pours in: what you are, what was done to you, the state of your body in that second, the long training that shaped where your attention even tends to go. The choosing is not free of cause; a choice cut loose from all of that would be the uncaused event this chapter already set aside, the severed island that cannot be. What is live is not the absence of cause. It is that the resolving happens here, now, at the fork, and not somewhere before it — the current arriving at a point and becoming definite there, in the present, which is the only place anything ever happens. Conditioned to the root, and still settled live. Both at once.

So is the choice determined, or is it free? Here the chapter’s own answer comes back, and it is the tie. From inside the moment, “the whole flow fixed this” and “I settled it, live, just now” are not two findings you could ever tell apart. They are one event, read from two distances. The one who could stand outside and see whether the fork was sealed before you reached it — that one does not exist, for the same reason no one can trace the hidden causes. So the choice is as determined as anything in this chapter and as free as anything ever is, and the two are not rivals. They are the inside and the outside of a single resolving, and you only ever get the inside.

And there is a will at every scale, and they are not all yours to place. The body has its pull, older and deeper than your attention. The long currents that carry a life — the language you think in, the conditioning you did not choose and cannot fully see — move you in ways you do not author, more a will you undergo than one you exercise. And the largest current of all, the whole turning itself — whether that, in any sense, wills, the way you do — is the question this book has refused from the start, and refuses here too. This much is enough, and honest: you choose, at your own scale, live, at the fork; and you are moved, at the scales above you, by currents you did not set going and cannot fully see. From inside, you cannot always sort which is which. That not-sorting is not a flaw in the account. It is the same open hand the whole chapter has been holding out.

We said the box was never there in space. We have now followed it into time, and found that an uncaused event would be a box in time — and that the same axiom forbids it, though it cannot yet prove the forbidding. Next we turn from cause to time itself: not whether events are determined, but how the past, the present, and the future are made — and why only one of the three is ever the place where anything actually happens.
